# Understanding Your Results & Graphs

This guide explains exactly what every file in your `results/` folder means. You can use this to quickly grasp what each graph or CSV proves so you can confidently explain them during your presentation.

---

## 📊 1. The Core Graphs (Your Visual Proof)

These images are the most important assets for your presentation. They visually prove the results of your evaluation.

### `rouge_vs_bertscore.png` (The Disagreement Chart )
- **What it shows:** A direct comparison between ROUGE-1 scores and BERTScore-F1 for every individual summary in your dataset.
- **What it proves:** You will see specific points (the "Paraphrase" summaries) where ROUGE drops incredibly low, but BERTScore stays very high. This is the visual proof that ROUGE penalizes paraphrasing while BERTScore understands semantic meaning.

### `hybrid_comparison.png` (The Innovation Chart)
- **What it shows:** A comparison of your custom "Hybrid Metric" against standard ROUGE and BERTScore across different dataset samples.
- **What it proves:** It visually demonstrates how your Hybrid Metric provides a "smoothed out" and more balanced evaluation. By taking the best of both lexical (ROUGE) and semantic (BERTScore) features, it doesn't fail as drastically on edge cases.

### `heatmap_pearson.png` (The Correlation Grid)
- **What it shows:** A color-coded grid mapping how closely different metrics agree with one another (from 0 to 1). A score close to 1 means they perfectly agree.
- **What it proves:** 
  - ROUGE-1 and ROUGE-2 will be highly correlated together (they are both word-matchers).
  - BERTScore and ROUGE will have a **moderate** correlation (~0.7). This means they agree on the absolute best and worst summaries, but disagree heavily in the middle (where the paraphrasing happens).

### `disagreement_analysis.png` (The Error Hunter)
- **What it shows:** A scatter plot marking exactly where the metrics disagree the most. It groups points into categories like "Semantic Match (ROUGE Fails)" or "Agreement-High".
- **What it proves:** This highlights the output of your Error Analysis Module. It visually classifies the specific summaries where the traditional metric failed, making it easy for a human reviewer to audit just the problematic summaries.

### `feature_importance.png` & `predicted_vs_actual.png` (The Machine Learning Charts)
- **What it shows:** 
  - **Feature Importance:** A bar chart showing which metrics the machine learning model relied on the most. (BERTScore-F1 will be the biggest bar).
  - **Predicted vs Actual:** A chart showing how accurately your Ridge Regression Machine Learning model predicted the final proxy score compared to the actual calculated score.
- **What it proves:** It proves that a mathematical, machine-learning-driven combination of metrics (with a high $R^2$ accuracy) is much more reliable than relying on just a single metric.

### `scatter_matrix.png` & `distributions.png`
- **What it shows:** Advanced statistical charts. `distributions` shows the bell curves (KDE) of how scores are spread out. `scatter_matrix` shows a pair-by-pair correlation of everything.
- **What it proves:** Mostly for the heavy academic report, proving that your dataset has a healthy and normal statistical distribution of scores.

## 📁 2. The Raw Data (The CSVs)

These files contain the exact mathematical numbers generated by running the test.

### `evaluation_results.csv`
- **The specific raw scores.** If you open this in Excel, you will see a row for every single test case (DUC2004_001 to 020) and columns for their ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, BERTScore, and BARTScore numbers. 

### `results_enriched.csv`
- **Your innovation data.** This is the same as the file above, but it appends the calculations for your **Hybrid Score** and labels them with the **Error Category** (e.g., "Semantic Match").

### `pearson_correlation.csv` & `spearman_correlation.csv`
- **The exact math behind the heatmap.** These text files contain the specific correlation matrices. This is where you pull the fact that ROUGE and BERTScore correlate at around `~0.70`.

If you only have time to show **two graphs**, show:

2. **`hybrid_comparison.png`**: To prove that your custom solution works better.
